# AICard Manifesto

## The idea

Most people are not programmers. But most people follow instructions every day — recipes, assembly guides, step-by-step tutorials. They understand sequences. They understand "first do this, then do that, unless this happens."

The gap between "following instructions" and "building automations" is not a gap in intelligence. It is a gap in metaphor.

AICard closes that gap. It gives ordinary people a kitchen. In that kitchen, they have recipes and cards. They follow a recipe. The sous chef helps. Things happen.

**If you can follow a recipe, you can build with AI.**

## The metaphor, explained

A **recipe** is a sequence of steps toward a purpose. "Send a thank you message to every new customer, three days after their order."

A **card** is a type of action a recipe can take. Like a kitchen tool — each one does one thing. Listen. Wait. Send a message.

The **kitchen** is the workspace. It contains your equipment (the services and accounts you've connected), your pantry (the cards available to you), and your recipes.

**Equipment** is a connected service — a shop, an email account, a spreadsheet. Equipment makes certain cards work. A "Send Message" card needs email equipment.

The **sous chef** is the AI collaborator. Not a chatbot. A quiet presence. It checks your kitchen before you run a recipe. It taps you on the shoulder when something goes wrong. It speaks in kitchen language.

## Who this is for

This is for Maria, who runs a small shop and knows what she wants to happen but not how to make it happen.

This is for Jun, who can figure things out but doesn't want to spend a weekend learning a new platform.

This is not — at first — for Sam, the developer who wants full control. Sam will find value in AICard eventually. But we are not building for Sam first.

## What we are not building

We are not building a no-code tool that secretly requires coding thinking.

We are not building a visual flowchart editor that calls itself a recipe.

We are not building an AI assistant that asks "what would you like to do today?" with a blank input box.

We are building a kitchen.

## The five levels

**Level 1 — Following.** Open a recipe someone else wrote. Run it. Watch it work.

**Level 2 — Tweaking.** Change a setting in a recipe. Make it yours without rewriting it.

**Level 3 — Combining.** Take two recipes and connect them. Build something new from existing parts.

**Level 4 — Creating.** Write a recipe from scratch. Choose your cards. Define your purpose.

**Level 5 — Inventing.** Build a new card type. Extend the kitchen.

Most people will live at Levels 1 and 2. That is not a failure. That is the design.

## The design tests

Before shipping anything, ask:

- **The shopkeeper test** — could Maria use this without knowing what's happening under the hood?
- **The text editor test** — can you open a recipe file in any text editor and understand it?
- **The sous chef test** — does every user-facing message use recipe language, not technical language?
- **The Sam test** — is there real substance here, or just a friendly label on a technical operation?

Pass all four or go back to the kitchen.
