# The AICard Manifesto

**A simple tool enabling endless creativity**

---

## The Gap

Something is broken in how we build with AI.

The models are extraordinary. They can reason, create, connect, and act. But look at the interface we give people: a text box. Type a message, get a message back. It's like handing someone a telephone and calling it a workshop.

We solved this problem once before. In 1987, Bill Atkinson shipped HyperCard with every Macintosh. It wasn't marketed as a programming environment. It was a tool for making things. Teachers made interactive lessons. Scientists made lab notebooks. Kids made adventure games. Librarians made catalog systems. None of these people called themselves developers. They were people with ideas who found a tool that respected their intelligence.

Then Apple killed it, because they couldn't figure out how to charge for it.

We believe it's time to try again — not by recreating HyperCard, but by carrying its principles forward into the age of AI. And this time, no one gets to kill it.

---

## What HyperCard Understood

HyperCard succeeded because of four design insights that the software industry has spent thirty years forgetting:

**The artifact is the tool.** There was no separation between using something and building something. You didn't "write code that generates an interface." You clicked on a button, opened its script, and told it what to do. The thing you saw was the thing you changed.

**Power reveals itself gradually.** HyperCard had five user levels: browsing, typing, painting, authoring, scripting. Each level opened new capabilities without invalidating what you already knew. You never felt stupid. You felt invited.

**Space, not time.** Stacks were places. Cards were things you could see, arrange, and point at. This is fundamentally different from a conversation, which is a river — everything flows past and is gone. You can't build in a river.

**Constraints are the feature.** HyperTalk read like English but was a real language with real structure. It wasn't "say anything and hope the computer understands." It was a learnable, reliable vocabulary tied to the objects on screen. The boundaries made it trustworthy.

## What AICard Adds: Human First, Machine Second

HyperCard gave us the design principles. AI gives us the power. But power without the right metaphor is just complexity. The automation tools that exist today — the workflow builders, the integration platforms — they put nodes on a canvas and call it simple. It isn't. Open any of them and within minutes you're staring at HTTP methods, JSON paths, and authentication types. They were built from the machine up. Technical operations first, human understanding as an afterthought.

AICard is built from the opposite direction. **Human first, machine second.** Every layer of the system — from how a card looks, to how you configure it, to how you share what you've made — is designed for human comprehension first and machine execution second. The AI handles the translation between what you mean and what the machine needs. You should never have to think like a computer to make one work for you.

This isn't just a philosophy. It's a concrete design test we apply to every decision: **can a person who has never heard of APIs, JSON, or webhooks build something useful with this?** If yes, we're on track. If not, we've failed — no matter how powerful the tool is underneath.

## Cards Are Ingredients

Here is where AICard departs from everything that came before.

In most automation tools, a node on the canvas is a technical operation wearing a label. In AICard, a card is an **ingredient** — a single, focused capability with a clear identity that anyone can understand. One card listens. One card summarizes. One card waits. One card sends. One card decides. You know what a card does the same way you know what garlic does. You don't need to understand the chemistry to use it.

Each card type has a small, well-defined set of things you can configure — not technical parameters, but choices that make sense in the domain. A "summarize" card lets you set the length and the tone. A "filter" card lets you describe what to keep and what to ignore. A "draft response" card asks about your voice and style. The AI works inside each card to fulfill its purpose, but you never have to prompt the AI directly. You are working with ingredients, not with code.

AICard ships with a core pantry — a curated set of card types that cover the essential capabilities: listening, filtering, summarizing, transforming, deciding, waiting, storing, and sending. This pantry is the shared vocabulary of AICard. It is small enough to learn, large enough to be useful, and open enough to grow. Because the community can create new card types — new ingredients — that everyone can use in their own recipes.

The constraints of each card type are not limitations. They are what make the system learnable, predictable, and trustworthy. You don't have to wonder what a card might do. Its type tells you what it does. Its configuration tells you how. If you can read a label, you can understand a card.

## Recipes, Not Programs

Cards are ingredients. So what do you make with them?

**Recipes.**

An AICard recipe is a combination of cards arranged in steps to achieve a purpose. Not a workflow. Not a pipeline. Not a directed graph. A recipe — the oldest format for sharing practical knowledge that humanity has.

The metaphor is not decorative. It is structural, and it carries deep meaning.

A recipe has a **purpose** — you know what it makes before you start. "Customer follow-up." "Weekly news digest." "Homework helper." The name tells you what you'll get.

A recipe has **ingredients** — the cards it uses, the connections it needs. You can see at a glance what's required, like scanning a recipe for items you might not have in your kitchen.

A recipe has **steps** — a sequence you follow. Not a flowchart. Steps. Humans have understood steps for thousands of years.

A recipe has an **expected result** — and you can tell whether it worked. Your output either matches what was promised or it doesn't.

And here is what matters most: **everyone already knows that recipes are meant to be adapted.** Nobody follows a recipe exactly. "The recipe says three days but I always wait five." "I changed the tone from formal to friendly because that's more my style." "I skipped the Slack notification because we use email." This isn't breaking the recipe. This is how recipes have always worked. Adaptation is not a feature we need to teach — it is a cultural practice people bring with them.

When you share an AICard recipe, you are sharing it the way your grandmother shares a recipe — here, I made this, try it, change it, make it yours. When someone opens your recipe, they see the ingredients, the steps, and the purpose. They can follow it exactly, or adjust it to their taste. The format is human-readable, the intent is clear, and the invitation to adapt is built into the metaphor itself.

## The Principles of AICard

AICard is built on these beliefs:

### 1. Creating is a human right, not a professional skill

The ability to build tools, automate tasks, and shape how technology works for you should not require a computer science degree. Every person has problems worth solving and ideas worth expressing. The gap between having an idea and making it real should be a gentle slope, not a cliff.

### 2. Human first, machine second

Every layer of AICard speaks human before it speaks machine. Cards describe capabilities in domain language, not technical operations. Configuration asks questions a person would understand, not parameters an engineer would set. Recipes read like instructions, not like code. The AI translates between your intent and the machine's needs. You never have to think like a computer.

### 3. The AI is the mortar, not the building

AI in AICard is not the product. It is the bridge between human intent and working logic. You place the ingredients — the cards, the connections, the configuration. The AI fills in the gaps, interprets your adjustments, and handles the complexity you shouldn't have to think about. The recipe is yours. The intelligence is a tool inside each card that makes your ingredients work.

### 4. Constraints enable creativity

AICard does not try to do "anything." Each card has a clear purpose. Each card type has a defined set of things you can configure. When you write instructions, they are grounded in the specific card you're working with — not thrown into a void. These constraints aren't limitations. They are what make the tool learnable, reliable, and trustworthy. Just as a kitchen with well-organized ingredients invites cooking, a canvas with well-defined cards invites creating.

### 5. See everything, hide nothing

Every AICard recipe is inspectable. You can always see what it does, what it connects to, what permissions it needs, and how it works. There are no black boxes. Each card shows its purpose and configuration in plain language. Trust comes from transparency, not from branding.

### 6. Offline first, cloud optional

AICard works without the internet. Small language models running locally via WebAssembly handle the AI inside each card. Cloud services and larger models are accelerators, not requirements. Your recipes are yours. They run on your machine. No subscription, no server, no permission needed.

### 7. Share like it's a family recipe

A recipe is a file. You share it by sending it. Someone else opens it and it works. No accounts, no platforms, no app stores. The format is open, documented, and human-readable. If AICard disappeared tomorrow, your recipes would still be usable text that any developer could interpret. And just like the best recipes, they get better as more people use them, adapt them, and pass them on.

### 8. Open source, forever

AICard is not a product. It is a public utility for human creativity. It is open source not as a business strategy but as a structural guarantee: no company can buy it, bury it, or put it behind a paywall. What happened to HyperCard will not happen here.

## The Five Levels

Like HyperCard, AICard meets people where they are and invites them deeper:

**Level 1 — Following.** You open a recipe someone shared with you. You press run. It does what it says. You don't need to understand how it works, just like you don't need to understand chemistry to follow a cooking recipe. You just use it.

**Level 2 — Tweaking.** You open a card and change its settings. "Make the tone more casual." "Send to Slack instead of email." "Wait five days instead of three." You are adjusting the recipe to your taste — the same way every cook adds a little more of this or a little less of that. No code, no scripting. Just making it yours.

**Level 3 — Combining.** You take cards from different recipes and arrange your own. Maybe you use the "listen for email" card from one recipe and the "summarize and send" sequence from another. You are combining known ingredients in a new way. The AI handles the plumbing between cards. You handle the creative decisions.

**Level 4 — Creating.** You write a recipe from scratch, choosing card types and configuring each one. For cards that need it, you add natural language instructions — but always within the card's defined purpose. "This is a filter card, and I want it to also catch messages that mention returns." You are an experienced cook who understands the ingredients well enough to invent new dishes.

**Level 5 — Inventing ingredients.** You create new card types. You define their purpose, their configuration options, their inputs and outputs. You write the underlying logic, the MCP connections, the API integrations. You are expanding the pantry — adding new ingredients that every AICard user can cook with.

Each level is complete in itself. You never need to go deeper than you want to. But the path is always open.

## Architecture for Openness

AICard's technical foundation serves its values:

**The open web is the platform.** AICard runs in any modern browser. No installation, no app store, no gatekeeper. WebAssembly provides the performance needed for local AI inference and the agent runtime. The browser is today's Macintosh — the universal platform everyone already has.

**Recipes are open files.** A recipe is a self-contained, human-readable document — plain, human-readable Markdown, saved as a `.recipe.md` file. You can open it in a text editor. You can version-control it. You can understand it without running it. Like SVG: rich, capable, and still just text.

**Cards describe intent, not implementation.** A card says "summarize this" or "classify this message" or "generate a response in my voice." Which AI model fulfills that intent is a runtime decision. Today it might be a local WASM model. Tomorrow it might be Claude, or Llama, or something that doesn't exist yet. The recipe doesn't care. Cards describe *what*, not *how*.

**MCP is the connectivity layer.** The Model Context Protocol provides the standard interface between AICard recipes and the outside world — email, calendars, databases, APIs, file systems. When a card says "search my email," it doesn't need to know about OAuth flows and REST endpoints. It needs an MCP server for email. MCP is the USB-C of AI tool use, and AICard plugs into it natively.

**Local-first, distributed by nature.** Your recipes live on your device. They can sync, share, and collaborate through whatever mechanism you choose — a file share, a Git repository, a USB stick. AICard doesn't need a cloud to function. It doesn't need *our* servers. It is software you own and control.

## What People Will Cook Up

We don't know. That's the point.

But we can imagine:

A teacher builds a recipe that takes a photo of a student's math work, identifies where the student went wrong, and generates an encouraging explanation in language appropriate for their age. Three cards. She shares it with her department. Another teacher tweaks the explanation style for younger students and shares that version back.

A neighborhood association builds a recipe that reads incoming community messages, classifies them by type and urgency, routes them to the right volunteer, and sends an acknowledgment. Nobody in the association writes code. They arranged cards on a screen and configured them in plain Dutch.

A small shop owner builds a three-card follow-up recipe: when a new order arrives, wait three days, then send a personalized thank-you written in their own voice, then log the interaction. No Zapier subscription. No developer. Just ingredients, arranged into steps.

A teenager builds a creative writing recipe that prompts them with story scenarios, critiques their drafts with the tone of their favorite author, and maintains a persistent world they're building — shared with friends who each add their own flavor.

A retired engineer builds a home automation recipe that monitors weather and energy prices and adjusts their systems accordingly, with each decision visible as a card they can inspect and adjust. Their neighbor asks for a copy and tweaks the thresholds for their own house.

All of these are technically possible today — with code, with platforms, with subscriptions, with developers. They will not be built that way. The barrier is too high and the tools speak the wrong language. AICard exists to lower that barrier to where the ideas are.

## Why Open Source Is Not Optional

HyperCard's death is a cautionary tale with a simple lesson: **a tool that empowers everyone to create will always be undervalued by companies that need to capture value.**

A commercial entity building AICard would inevitably need lock-in, proprietary formats, subscription revenue, and controlled distribution. Every one of these needs would erode exactly the qualities that make the tool work: zero-friction sharing, universal access, user ownership, and the "here, try my recipe" culture that turns a tool into a movement.

Open source is not a nice-to-have for AICard. It is a structural requirement. It is the mechanism by which we guarantee that:

- No company can acquire and discontinue it
- No format change can hold your recipes hostage
- No pricing model can exclude anyone from creating
- The community of builders owns what they build — the recipes, the card types, and AICard itself

We build this in the open, for the commons, because that is the only architecture that aligns with the mission.

## The Invitation

Computing was supposed to be a bicycle for the mind. For most people, it became a bus — you ride where it takes you, on someone else's schedule, along someone else's route.

AI could make this worse. It could become the ultimate bus: faster, more capable, and more opaque than ever. You type what you want, the machine decides what you get, and you're grateful for the ride.

Or AI could be the thing that finally gives everyone their own bicycle.

AICard is a bet on the second future. A bet that ordinary people are capable of extraordinary things when given the right tool. A bet that if you can follow a recipe, you can build with AI. A bet that the right metaphor isn't a conversation with an oracle but a card on a canvas — something you can see, touch, arrange, share, and make your own.

HyperCard showed us this was possible. The web gives us the platform. AI gives us the power. Open source gives us the permanence.

All that's left is to build it.
